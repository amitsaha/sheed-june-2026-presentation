The Input (Left): "On the last slide, we saw that a clinical image is just a grid of numbers. Now, we feed those numbers into the Artificial Neural Network—specifically, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) designed for images."
Layer 1 (The First Math Filters): "The AI doesn't look at the whole picture at once. In 'Layer 1', it applies simple mathematical filters to our numbers. At this stage, the AI is completely blind to biology; it is just looking for sharp changes in numbers that indicate a physical edge, a line, or a color gradient."
What is 'Learning'?: "Look at the red box on the slide. When we say an AI 'Learns', there is no magic involved. 'Learning' simply means the computer automatically adjusts the mathematical weights of these filters over millions of examples until it successfully identifies the right biological patterns."
The N-Layers (The 'Deep' in Deep Learning): "Why do we call it Deep Learning? Because between the first layer and the end, there are 'N' number of hidden layers (sometimes 50 to 100 layers deep). Layer 1 finds edges. Layer 5 combines those edges into circles. Layer 20 combines those circles into recognizable cell structures or abnormal tissue architectures."
Final Layer to Prediction (Right): "By the time the data reaches the final layer, the AI has distilled millions of pixels into a few key numerical biomarkers. It then uses simple statistics to output a final clinical probability—for example, determining with 88% confidence that the mathematical patterns match abnormal pathology."
